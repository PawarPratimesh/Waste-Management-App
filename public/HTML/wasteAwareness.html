2. Types of Waste

Understanding waste categories is the first step toward proper disposal:

Organic Waste

Biodegradable items like food scraps, leaves, and garden waste.

Recyclables

Paper, cardboard, metals, certain plastics, and glass.

Hazardous Waste

Batteries, paints, cleaning chemicals, e-waste.

E-Waste

Phones, computers, and electronic devices.

Non-Recyclables

Contaminated materials, thermocol, chip packets, etc.

Each type needs a distinct approach. For example, e-waste should never be thrown in general dustbins‚Äîit contains lead and mercury, which can leach into soil and water.

4. Waste Segregation

Segregating waste at the source is key. Use color-coded bins to simplify:

Green Bin

Organic waste (kitchen scraps, peels)

Blue Bin

Dry recyclables (paper, plastic, metal)

Red Bin

Hazardous waste (e-waste, chemicals)

Black Bin

Reject/Non-recyclables

Tip: Label bins at home/school clearly. Even a 10% increase in segregation accuracy reduces landfill stress drastically.

5. The 3Rs Principle

A simple but powerful framework:

Reduce

  • Avoid unnecessary consumption.
  • Carry reusable bags and bottles.
  • Choose items with minimal packaging.

Reuse

  • Find new life for old items.
  • Upcycle jars into planters.
  • Donate old clothes instead of discarding.

Recycle

  • Convert old into new.
  • Sort and clean recyclables.
  • Support recycled products (e.g., recycled paper notebooks).

Fact: Recycling 1 ton of paper saves 17 trees and 7,000 gallons of water.

6. Composting Basics

Composting is nature's way of recycling organics into healthy soil.

How It Works:

  • Mix green (wet) and brown (dry) waste.
  • Maintain moisture and turn the pile regularly.
  • Avoid dairy, meat, and oily foods.
  • Within 2‚Äì6 months, your waste turns into compost.
